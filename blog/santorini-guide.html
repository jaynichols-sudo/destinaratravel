Santorini, Greece: The Complete Island Guide

By Elizabeth Nichols | June 2025 | 10 min read

There's a reason Santorini appears on nearly every travel bucket list in the world. The dramatic caldera views, the whitewashed villages perched on volcanic cliffs, the sunsets that seem almost too beautiful to be real — this island delivers on every promise. And yet, it can also overwhelm first-time visitors who arrive without a plan.

I've been to Santorini three times now, and each visit has taught me something new about how to experience it well. The key isn't just knowing what to see — it's knowing when to see it, where to stay, and which crowds to avoid. This guide is everything I wish I'd known on my first trip.

01 — When to Visit

Timing Your Trip Right

Santorini's tourist season runs from April through October, but the island is not equally enjoyable throughout that window. July and August are brutally hot and crowded — cruise ships dump thousands of visitors into Oia each afternoon, and every restaurant requires a reservation made weeks in advance.

My recommendation: go in May, early June, or September. The weather is gorgeous, the caldera views are just as stunning, and you can actually walk through Oia without being pressed shoulder-to-shoulder. Late September and early October are arguably the best value months on the island — warm enough to swim, cool enough to hike, and visibly quieter.

Advisor Tip — Elizabeth Nichols

If your heart is set on the famous Oia sunset, arrive at least 90 minutes early in peak season — the viewing spots fill completely. In shoulder season (May or September), 30 minutes is usually enough. The Firostefani section of the caldera path offers equally spectacular sunsets with far smaller crowds.

02 — The Villages

Choosing Your Base: Oia, Fira, or Imerovigli?

Where you stay shapes your entire Santorini experience. The three main caldera villages each have a distinct character, and understanding the differences will help you choose wisely.

Oia is the postcard village — the iconic blue-domed churches, the narrow whitewashed lanes, the world-famous sunset point. It's the most photographed and most crowded part of the island. Staying here puts you in the middle of the beauty but also the chaos. If you choose Oia, pick a property that faces the caldera so you can watch the sunset from your terrace rather than fighting the crowds at the viewpoints.

Fira is the island's capital and most practical base — it has the widest selection of restaurants, shops, and nightlife. The caldera views are excellent, and it's well-connected to the rest of the island. It lacks Oia's fairy-tale feel, but it's far more livable for a week-long stay.

Imerovigli sits between Oia and Fira on the caldera rim and is, in my opinion, the sweet spot. It's quieter than both neighbors, the views are breathtaking, and the sunset rivals Oia's. Several of the island's finest cave hotels are located here.

Villages at a Glance

  • Oia — Most scenic, most crowded, great for honeymoons and short stays
  • Fira — Best practical base, widest options, good nightlife access
  • Imerovigli — Quietest caldera village, stunning views, romantic atmosphere
  • Pyrgos — Inland hilltop village, far from crowds, excellent wine country access
  • Perissa & Perivolos — Black sand beach towns on the eastern coast, most affordable
03 — Where to Stay

Accommodation: What to Know Before You Book

Santorini accommodation falls into a few distinct categories, and your choice matters enormously. The iconic cave hotels carved into the caldera cliffs are extraordinary — many feature private plunge pools that seem to float over the water thousands of feet below. These properties book out 8–12 months in advance for peak season and command premium prices, but they deliver an experience unlike anywhere else in the world.

If a cave hotel isn't in the budget, look for caldera-view properties in Imerovigli or Fira that offer shared infinity pools overlooking the water. The view is nearly as spectacular at a fraction of the price. For beach-focused travelers, the villages of Perissa and Perivolos on the southeastern coast offer good-value hotels steps from Santorini's distinctive black sand beaches.

Booking Advice

Don't book a "caldera view" room without confirming what that view actually is. Some properties advertise caldera views that are partially obstructed or face a less dramatic section of the cliff. Ask the hotel for photos from your specific room category, or search for guest photos on review sites before committing.

04 — What to Do

Beyond the Sunset: Experiences Worth Seeking Out

Most visitors to Santorini come for the views and the sunsets, but the island has far more to offer. The caldera was formed by one of the largest volcanic eruptions in recorded history, and that geological drama is woven into everything from the landscape to the wine.

A sailing trip around the caldera is, in my view, the single best way to spend a day on the island. Catamaran tours typically include snorkeling at the hot springs near the volcanic island of Nea Kameni, a swim at the red and white beaches accessible only by sea, and lunch and wine on deck with the caldera cliffs rising on all sides. It's genuinely spectacular.

Santorini's wine scene is also genuinely exceptional. The island produces Assyrtiko, a crisp, mineral-driven white wine grown in ancient basket-trained vines that have survived for centuries in the volcanic soil. Visiting a winery like Domaine Sigalas or Estate Argyros for a tasting is one of the most memorable afternoons on the island — especially at sunset.

Experiences Not to Miss

  • Catamaran sailing tour around the caldera with snorkeling and lunch
  • Wine tasting at a caldera-view winery (Assyrtiko is the local grape to seek out)
  • Hike the ancient path from Fira to Oia (8km, ~3 hours, best done at sunrise)
  • Visit Akrotiri — a remarkably preserved Minoan city buried by the volcanic eruption c. 1627 BC
  • Swim at the Red Beach (accessible by water taxi from Akrotiri village)
  • Explore Pyrgos village and its Venetian-era castle at dusk
05 — Pairing With Athens or Island-Hopping

Building a Broader Greece Itinerary

Santorini is best experienced as part of a longer Greece trip rather than as a standalone destination. The island's small size means 3–4 days is genuinely sufficient to experience its highlights — staying longer than that often leads to the same restaurants and viewpoints on repeat.

The classic pairing is Athens plus Santorini: 3 nights in Athens to cover the Acropolis, the museums, and the Plaka neighborhood, then fly or ferry to Santorini for 3–4 nights. This is a highly satisfying itinerary for a first visit to Greece and can be accomplished in 8–9 days total.

For a more adventurous trip, consider island-hopping through the Cyclades: Athens → Mykonos (2 nights) → Paros or Naxos (2 nights) → Santorini (3 nights). The ferry connections are excellent in summer, and each island has a completely distinct character. Naxos in particular is often overlooked — it's the largest Cycladic island, has beautiful beaches, and feels far more authentic than its more famous neighbors.

Getting There & Around

Santorini (JTR) has direct international flights from many European hubs in summer, and connects to Athens (ATH) with multiple daily flights (~45 min). High-speed ferries from Athens' Piraeus port take 5–8 hours depending on route. On the island, renting an ATV or small car is the most flexible way to explore — the bus system is good for the main caldera route but limited for beaches and wineries.
