Your First African Safari: A Complete Beginner's Guide

By Jay & Elizabeth Nichols • Destinara Travel

Home › Blog › Your First African Safari
🌍 Africa Travel 📅 Published March 2026 ⏱️ 12 min read

There is nothing quite like your first African safari. The moment a lion emerges from the tall grass, or a herd of elephants crosses your path at sunset, time seems to stop. It's one of those rare travel experiences that resets your perspective on the natural world — and on yourself.

After guiding dozens of clients on their first-ever safaris across Kenya, Tanzania, Botswana, and South Africa, we've learned that the difference between a good safari and a truly life-changing one comes down to planning. This guide covers everything you need to know.

Choosing Your Safari Country

Africa is vast — nearly four times the size of the United States — and each country offers a distinct safari experience. Here's a quick breakdown of the most popular destinations:

🦁 Kenya

Home of the Great Migration and the iconic Masai Mara. Best for first-timers who want guaranteed wildlife density and iconic landscapes. Peak season: July–October.

🐘 Tanzania

The Serengeti, Ngorongoro Crater, and Zanzibar in one trip. World-class wildlife and a more remote feel. Best combined with Kenya for the full Migration experience.

🦒 Botswana

Ultra-exclusive, low-volume safari with extraordinary wildlife. The Okavango Delta is one of Earth's last great wild places. Premium pricing, unmatched intimacy.

🦏 South Africa

Malaria-free options in the Karoo, Big Five in Kruger, and world-class Cape Town. Ideal if you want to pair wildlife with wine country and beaches.

Destinara Tip: For most first-time safari guests, we recommend Kenya's Masai Mara for July–October (Migration season), or the Greater Kruger in South Africa year-round for a malaria-free option.

When Is the Best Time to Go?

The short answer: the dry season is almost always better for game viewing. When water sources are scarce, animals congregate around rivers and waterholes — making them far easier to find and photograph.

  • East Africa (Kenya/Tanzania): July–October for the Great Migration and best game viewing. January–March for calving season — dramatic predator action.
  • Southern Africa (Botswana/South Africa): May–September for dry season game viewing. April–May is the "emerald season" — lush and less crowded.
  • Avoid: Peak rainy seasons can make roads impassable and reduce wildlife sightings significantly.

Choosing the Right Camp or Lodge

Where you stay defines your safari experience. African lodges range from basic tented camps to ultra-luxury properties where the Big Five might wander through camp while you're having dinner. Here's what to know:

Mobile Tented Camps

Authentic, immersive, and often positioned right in the heart of prime game areas. These camps move seasonally to follow the wildlife. Spartan but magical — you fall asleep to the sounds of lions roaring and hyenas calling.

Luxury Fixed Lodges

Properties like &Beyond's Ngorongoro Crater Lodge or Singita Grumeti offer extraordinary wildlife with world-class amenities — infinity pools overlooking game plains, Michelin-level cuisine, and private game drives. Perfect if comfort matters as much as wildlife.

Private Conservancies

Found around Masai Mara and in Botswana, private conservancies allow off-road driving and night game drives that aren't permitted in national parks. For serious wildlife enthusiasts, this makes a significant difference.

Destinara Tip: Book your lodge at least 6–12 months in advance for peak season dates. The best camps sell out very early — especially July–October in Kenya and August in Botswana.

What to Expect on a Game Drive

A typical safari day starts before sunrise. Your guide will wake you at 5:30 AM for a quick coffee, then you'll head out in an open-sided Land Rover as the sun rises over the savanna. This golden hour is when predators are most active and the light is spectacular for photography.

You'll typically spend 3–4 hours on a morning drive, return to camp for breakfast and a midday rest (it's hot and animals rest too), then head out again at 4 PM for the afternoon drive. Sundowners — drinks in the bush at sunset — are a beloved tradition.

Don't be disappointed if you don't see everything on day one. Experienced guides know their territory intimately, and good things come to those who are patient. Some of the most memorable safari moments happen while waiting — and watching.

What to Pack

Safari packing is deceptively specific. Here are the essentials:

  • Neutral colors only — khaki, olive, beige, grey. Never bright colors or white, which attract insects and disturb animals.
  • Layers — early mornings are surprisingly cold even in summer. A fleece or light down jacket is essential.
  • Wide-brimmed hat and quality sunscreen — you'll be outside in the African sun for hours.
  • Binoculars — 8x42 or 10x42 are the sweet spot. Transformative for distant wildlife.
  • Camera with a telephoto lens — your phone is fine for general shots, but a 100–400mm lens changes everything.
  • Insect repellent with DEET — especially important in malaria-endemic areas.
  • Soft bags only — most small aircraft have strict weight limits (15kg/33lbs). Hard suitcases are often not permitted.

Health & Safety

A well-planned safari is remarkably safe. Here's what to know:

Malaria: Kenya, Tanzania, Botswana, and Mozambique are malaria-endemic. Consult your doctor about antimalarials at least 6 weeks before travel. South Africa's Kruger National Park is also malaria-endemic; however, the Karoo and Western Cape are malaria-free alternatives.

Vaccinations: Yellow fever vaccination is required for entry to some countries and recommended for others. Check current requirements with your doctor and the embassy of each country you'll visit.

Wildlife encounters: Your guides are professionals who know animal behavior intimately. Follow their instructions at all times — never stand up in the vehicle, never leave the vehicle without permission, and never approach animals on foot without a guide.

Destinara Tip: Travel insurance with emergency medical evacuation coverage is non-negotiable for safari. Medical facilities in remote bush areas are minimal — evacuation is the standard response to serious health emergencies.

How to Choose a Safari Operator

The quality of your guide is the single most important factor in your safari experience. A great guide can make a mediocre game area extraordinary; a poor guide can make the Serengeti disappointing. When evaluating operators:

  • Ask how long their guides have been working in that specific ecosystem
  • Look for operators who use local guides rather than imported staff
  • Check if they operate their own camps or if they're booking third-party properties (both are fine, but transparency matters)
  • Read reviews specifically about the guides, not just the facilities
  • Ask about their conservation commitments — responsible operators reinvest in the wildlife they depend upon

The Ethical Dimension

Safari tourism, done right, is one of the most powerful forces for wildlife conservation on the planet. When local communities earn more from live animals than from poaching, protection happens naturally. When you choose an ethical operator who employs local staff, sources food locally, and contributes to conservation funds, your tourism dollars create a direct incentive to protect the ecosystems you came to experience.

Look for operators who are members of the Africa Travel & Tourism Association (ATTA), who are carbon-neutral certified, and who partner with established conservation organizations like AWF or African Wildlife Foundation.
